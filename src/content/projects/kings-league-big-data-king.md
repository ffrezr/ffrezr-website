## Football reimagined, data included

The **Kings League** is not your typical football competition. Created by **Gerard Piqué** in 2022, it's a 7-a-side league designed for the streaming era — 40-minute matches, unlimited substitutions, and entertainment-first mechanics like "secret weapon" cards that can double the value of a goal, trigger automatic penalties, or temporarily remove a player from the pitch. What started as an experiment in Barcelona quickly grew into a global phenomenon, expanding across Spain, the Americas, Italy, and beyond.

But behind the spectacle, there was a data problem: the league generates an enormous volume of match footage, and extracting meaningful performance metrics from those recordings was still largely a manual effort. That's where our team came in.

## The challenge

Traditional sports analytics relies heavily on human annotators — people who watch game footage frame by frame, tagging events like passes, shots, fouls, and player positions. It's accurate, but painfully slow and expensive. The Kings League, with its rapid match cadence and growing number of fixtures across multiple countries, needed a faster, scalable approach.

The goal was clear: **automate the extraction of match performance metrics directly from video recordings** using Computer Vision and Deep Learning. Instead of a room full of analysts manually logging events, we wanted neural networks doing the heavy lifting — identifying players, tracking the ball, detecting key game events, and outputting structured data ready for analysis.

## The team and my role

I joined a small, focused team of data scientists and engineers working remotely with the Kings League operation based in Barcelona. Our mandate was narrow but technically demanding: build and refine **Computer Vision pipelines** capable of processing match recordings and extracting actionable metrics at scale.

My work centered on developing and training **neural network models** for object detection and tracking — identifying players, the ball, and key events within the video feed. This involved experimenting with architectures suited for real-time sports video analysis, handling the particular challenges of a 7-a-side format (smaller pitch, faster play, more occlusion), and iterating on model performance against manually annotated ground truth data.

The tech stack was Python-centric, leveraging established deep learning frameworks for model training and inference, combined with video processing libraries for frame extraction and pre-processing.

## Why it mattered

For me, this project sat at the intersection of two things I care about deeply: **Computer Vision** — the field where I cut my teeth during my years at Electroveja Labs and UTFSM — and the kind of fast-moving, unconventional venture that the Kings League represents. It was a chance to apply the same neural network skills I'd used for museum installations and interactive art to a completely different domain: competitive sports.

Automating metrics capture isn't just about efficiency. It unlocks a layer of analytics that wasn't previously feasible — giving coaches, teams, and the league itself access to richer, more granular data about player performance, tactical patterns, and match dynamics. In a league built for the digital age, having data infrastructure to match felt like a natural evolution.

## The takeaway

The Kings League project reinforced something I'd learned at Electroveja: Computer Vision is at its most exciting when applied to messy, real-world video — not clean lab datasets. Sports footage is noisy, fast, and full of edge cases. Training models that perform reliably in those conditions demands both technical rigor and creative problem-solving. It was a short but intense engagement that sharpened my deep learning skills and gave me a front-row seat to the intersection of sports and AI.
